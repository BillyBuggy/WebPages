Overuse of "Growing Exponentially (4)"

Background: How quantities change

  • By addition           (absolute)
  • By multiplication (relative)

  • By addition (absolute)
      10 + 2 = 12,    12 + 2 = 14,    14 + 2 = 16,    etc.
      Last above:   10   +   2 + 2 + 2 = 16
      Generally:     10   +   3 x 2       = 16
                            10   +   k x 2
      Using mathematical subscripts

      \( \myIndentB x_{k+1} = x_{k} + 2 \)

      \( \myIndentB x_{k+1} = x_{0} + k \times 2 \)


  • By multiplication (relative)
      10 x 1.2 = 12,    12 x 1.2 = 14.4,    14.4 x 1.2 = 17.28,    etc.
      Last above:   10 x 1.2 x 1.2 x 1.2 = 17.28
      Generally:     10 x \( {1.2}^3 \)                 = 17.28
                            10 x \( {1.2}^k \)
      Using mathematical subscripts

      \( \myIndentB x_{k+1} = x_{k} \times 1.2 \)

      \( \myIndentB x_{k+1} = x_{0} \times 1.2^k \)


Background: "Exponential Change"

  • Relative change is called "Exponential Change"

  • The \( \mySize 1.2^k \)   in   \( \mySize x_{k+1} = x_{0} \times 1.2^k \) can be rewritten:

    \( \myIndentC y = 1.2^k \)

    \( \myIndentC ln(y) = ln(1.2^k) \)

    \( \myIndentA \mbox{Apply   } ln(b^c) = c \times ln(b) \)

    \( \myIndentC ln(y) = k \times ln(1.2) \)

    \( \myIndentA \mbox{Exponentiate both sides base  } e \)

    \( \myIndentC e ^ {ln(y)} = e ^ {k \times ln(1.2)} \)

    \( \myIndentA \mbox{Simplify using } e ^ {ln(y)} = y \)

    \( \myIndentC y = e ^ {k \times ln(1.2)} \)

    \( \myIndentA \mbox{Substitute back } y = 1.2^k \)

    \( \myIndentC 1.2^k = e ^ {k \times ln(1.2)} \)

  • Conclusion: Any change (e.g, the 1.2 or 20% growth above) can be viewed as or called "exponential change".

Absolute Change Examples

  • Height
  • OK:             "I grew by 1/2 inch"
  • STRANGE: "I grew by 10%"

  • Travel Distance
  • "Daddy, how far are we from grandma's house?"
  • OK:             "Another 50 miles"
  • STRANGE: "Another 20%"
  • STRANGE: "We're 80% there"

  • Progress Bar
  • Number printed can be in percent
  • But bar cannot grow logarithmically

           

Relative Change Has Many Facets

Changes that literally grow relatively

  • Examples
  • Virus spread: Each person infected spreads to 3.5 others
  • Population growth: Each person creates 1.25 kids
  • Fission: Each uranium nucleus produces multiple neutrons

  • OK:          Our population is growing annually by 1.5%
  • Not OK:   Our population is growing annually by 120,000

Changes that we perceive relatively

  • Weber's law
  • "The minimum increase of stimulus which will produce a
      perceptible increase of sensation is proportional to the
      pre-existing stimulus."

  • Hearing differences in volume
  • Can      distinguish    1     versus    2     pins dropping
  • Cannot distinguish 1001 versus 1002 pins dropping

  • Hearing differences in frequency
  • Can      distinguish 100     versus 103     Hz
  • Cannot distinguish 10001 versus 10003 Hz

Changes we choose to grow relatively

  • Number of iterations to measure cpu time
  • Run      100 iterations, report time.
  • Run    1000 iterations, report time.
  • Run 10,000 iterations, report time.

Changes we want to be reported relatively

  • Report relatively (in %) if growth mode actually is relative

  • Report in % if range is over 2 orders of magnitude
    eg: 1-100 or 1000-100,000 or 50-5000

    (If span > 2 or 3 orders of magnitude,
    growth mode probably is relative.


  • Stock price
  • OK:             "It's up by 6 points (6%)"
  • STRANGE: "It's up by 2 dollars"

  • Benefit of reporting relative change:
    don't have to know the actual value
    e.g.: can judge a stock w/o knowing its actual price
Main Point and Conclusions

  • People use "growing expontially" to instill fear of something
    "wildly out of control" due to plots like this:


  • If you use a log-log plot you see that exponential growth is routine:


  • If the multiplicative (exponential) increase didn't
